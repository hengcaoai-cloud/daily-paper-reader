# Abstract 

Learning from demonstration is a powerful method for robotic skill acquisition. However, the significant expense of collecting such action-labeled robot data presents a major bottleneck. Video data, a rich data source encompassing diverse behavioral and physical knowledge, emerges as a promising alternative. In this paper, we present GraphMimic ,a novel paradigm that leverages video data via graph-to-graphs generative modeling, which pre-trains models to gen-erate future graphs conditioned on the graph within a video frame. Specifically, GraphMimic abstracts video frames into object and visual action vertices, and constructs graphs for state representations. The graph generative modeling network then effectively models internal structures and spa-tial relationships within the constructed graphs, aiming to generate future graphs. The generated graphs serve as con-ditions for the control policy, mapping to robot actions. Our concise approach captures important spatial relations and enhances future graph generation accuracy, enabling the acquisition of robust policies from limited action-labeled data. Furthermore, the transferable graph representations facilitate the effective learning of manipulation skills from cross-embodiment videos. Our experiments exhibit that GraphMimic achieves superior performance using merely 20% action-labeled data. Moreover, our method outperforms the state-of-the-art method by over 17% and 23% in simula-tion and real-world experiments, and delivers improvements of over 33% in cross-embodiment transfer experiments.

Correspondingly, graph representations inherently encode spatial relational bias, enabling effective structural modeling. Furthermore, we can directly incorpo-rate visual action vertices into constructed graphs, capturing the relationships between objects and effectors. Inspired by the encouraging properties of graph representation, we natu-rally ask the question: Can we formulate video pre-training as graph-to-graphs generative modeling, enabling policy learning on limited action-labeled data? 

To answer this question, we exploit the graph-to-graphs generative modeling scheme for effective video pre-training. However, several challenges must be overcome: (I) The source video and target scene often exhibit substantial em-bodiment discrepancy, hindering the transfer of learned ac-tion knowledge. To address this issue, we propose the action-informed transferable graph representation, which extracts visual action vertices by abstracting key interaction points of the embodiment, facilitating cross-embodiment transfer. Fur-thermore, the representation incorporates vertices on relevant objects, independent of specific embodiments. (II) The prop-erties of distinct objects exhibit significant variability, such as stiffness, inhibiting the generative modeling network from capturing diverse object behaviors. To address this challenge, we propose to infer properties from historical observations and integrate them into graphs, facilitating predictions for objects with distinct properties. (III) Modeling long-range dependencies is crucial for capturing spatial relationships, but directly expanding edge construction range potentially overwhelms important internal structures. To optimize this trade-off, we present a hierarchical architecture, integrating global vertices for long-range information propagation, en-abling the effective modeling of important internal structures and spatial relationships. Based on the above analysis, we present GraphMimic , a novel approach that formulates video pre-training as graph-to-graphs generative modeling. GraphMimic abstracts video frames into objects and visual action vertices, and constructs action-informed transferable graphs for state representations, providing spatial relational bias while facilitating cross-embodiment transfer. Subsequently, the graph generative modeling network extracts object properties and establishes a hierarchical architecture to effectively model internal struc-tures and spatial relationships, accurately generating future graphs conditioned on the constructed graphs. The generated graphs, as shown in Figure 1, function as guidance for the control policy, facilitating the mapping to robotic actions. Our concise approach effectively captures important struc-tural information, improving graph generation accuracy and enabling robust policy learning from limited action-labeled data. Furthermore, the transferable graph representations facilitate the effective learning of manipulation skills from cross-embodiment videos. Our extensive experiments ex-hibit that our GraphMimic achieves superior performance with only 20% of the data required by baselines, and signifi-cantly outperforms them with identical data volumes. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: (I) We propose GraphMimic , a novel framework that bridges video pre-training to policy learning via graph-to-graphs gen-erative modeling. GraphMimic constructs action-informed transferable graphs within video frames, and generates future graphs to provide precise and expressive guidance. (II) We propose an object property estimation approach to facilitate behavior predictions for diverse objects, and introduce a hi-erarchical architecture to capture local structures and spatial relationships. (III) Extensive experiments spanning over 140 tasks demonstrate that our method surpasses the state-of-the-art method by more than 17% and 23% in simulation and real-world experiments, and exhibits over 33% enhancement in cross-embodiment transfer experiments. 

# 2. Related work 

## 2.1. Learning from Videos for Manipulation 

Learning from video data provides a promising paradigm to alleviate the reliance on expensive action-labeled robot data. To leverage easily accessible video data resources, multiple learning frameworks are proposed for robot learn-ing. A plethora of recent research [9], [10], [24]–[27] fo-cuses on learning task-relevant priors from videos to guide robot behaviors or derive a heuristic reward function for rein-forcement learning. These methods encounter challenges in bridging the sim-to-real gap and constructing task-specific environments. Another line of work [6], [28], [29] explores leveraging large-scale human video data to pretrain visual representations for subsequent imitation learning tasks. How-ever, a visual representation only describes the state at the current time step, largely neglecting the transition dynam-ics. More recently, several works [18]–[22], [30], [31] have explored video learning through video generation or flow generation. UniPi [18] proposes to leverage text-guided video generation to create versatile agents. ATM [21] lever-ages video demonstrations to pre-train a trajectory genera-tion model, bridging video pre-training and policy learning. However, these methods directly model representations from pixel space, neglecting the modeling of important structures.

# 5. Experiments 

Baselines . GraphMimic is compared with four repre-sentative methods: (1) BC represents vanilla behavioral cloning, which trains a policy solely on action-labeled ex-pert demonstrations. It uses a policy architecture identi-cal to GraphMimic , but excludes graph conditions and in-stead utilizes language embeddings for task specification; (2) R3M [7], which employs contrastive learning to align video and language representations. We adopt pre-trained Ego4D weights and fine-tune the weights on our video dataset, to ini-tialize the BC policy’s visual encoder; (3) UniPi [18], which trains a text-conditioned video diffusion model to generate fine-grained video plans, followed by an inverse dynamics model trained on the action-labeled data. (4) ATM [21], which trains a track generation model, and a corresponding track-guided policy for action prediction. The comparisons with Diffusion policy (DP) [61] and Im2Flow2Act [30] are provided in Appendix ?? and Appendix ?? .

Simulation experiment setup . Our evaluation utilizes the LIBERO benchmark [62], encompassing 130 language-conditioned manipulation tasks. This benchmark comprises five distinct suites. We compare with baselines on each suite separately. Baseline methods are trained on 10 action-labeled demonstration trajectories and 50 action-free videos of the robot for each task. For each task, we train each method with three different random seeds. 

Simulation experiment results . The performance of com-parison methods is summarized in Table 1, demonstrating that our method surpasses all other methods in all task suites. Our method, trained with only 2 action-labeled trajecto-ries for each task, outperforms the latest methods ATM, and UniPi, which employ five times as many action-labeled trajectories. Moreover, our GraphMimic achieves superior performance even when compared to BC trained on the complete dataset. When trained with 10 action-labeled tra-jectories for each task, our method significantly surpasses all baselines, achieving an improvement of over 17% .

Real-world experiment setup . We collect 10 action-labeled trajectories and 50 action-free demonstration videos for each task, and train policies on all training data using task descrip-tions as language conditions. Success criteria are human-evaluated and the success rate is calculated from 10 random-ized object positions and orientations. 

Real-world experiment results . Quantitative results in Ta-ble 2 illustrate GraphMimic consistently and significantly outperforms BC and other video pre-training baselines. Remarkably, GraphMimic maintains superior performance even when utilizing merely 20% of action-labeled data re-quired by baseline, consistently exceeding other video pre-training methods. Additionally, our approach exhibits robust failure recovery capabilities. We posit that the graph model-ing of topological relationships between objects and actions allows GraphMimic to learn general principles of object in-teractions, yielding a substantial performance enhancement.

# 6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we present GraphMimic , a novel paradigm that formulates video pre-training as graph-to-graphs gen-erative modeling. GraphMimic constructs graphs within video frames. Then, the graph generative modeling network extracts object properties and establishes a hierarchical ar-chitecture to effectively model local structures and spatial re-lationships, aiming to generate future graph sequences. The predicted graphs serve as conditions for the control policy, mapping to robot actions. Our concise approach effectively captures important structural information, enabling robust policy learning from limited action-labeled data. Moreover, the transferable graph representations facilitate the effec-tive learning of manipulation skills from cross-embodiment videos. Our extensive experiments across 140 tasks exhibit that our method outperforms the state-of-the-art method by over 17% and 23% in simulation and real-world manipu-lation tasks, and delivers substantial improvements of over 33% in cross-embodiment transfer experiments. 

Limitations . Despite the promising performance exhibited by GraphMimic , current SOTA tracking models still struggle with low-texture objects during large motions, compromising training data quality. We believe that the progression of tracking models will mitigate this limitation. Appendix ??
